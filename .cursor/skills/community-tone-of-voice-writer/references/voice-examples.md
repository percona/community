# Voice Examples

These are real examples of the Percona Community voice. Read them before writing to internalize
the tone, sentence rhythm, and how the writer addresses the reader.

---

## Example 1: Opinion / thought leadership article

**Title:** Your community isn't a funnel (and why you should build a ladder instead)

**Opening:**
> In the world of marketing, the "AARRRP" funnel is king. It is a sequence designed to move
> people through stages: Awareness to Acquisition, then Activation, Retention, Revenue, Referral,
> and finally, the Product. It is a clean, logical way to process leads.
>
> But here is the truth: Your community members are not leads.

**What makes this work:**
- Opens by naming a familiar framework — then challenges it immediately.
- "Here is the truth" signals a real opinion is coming.
- Short declarative after a longer setup. Deliberate contrast.

**Key passage:**
> On a community ladder, the very first rung is the Observer — or what we often call the "Lurker."
> Many community managers view lurkers as a problem to be "solved." They want to force them into
> the funnel to get them "activated." But we need to defend the lurker.

**What makes this work:**
- Takes a side without hedging.
- "Activated" in quotes signals skepticism about the word itself.
- Uses "we" to pull the reader into the argument, not lecture them.

## Example 2: Community page copy

**Title:** Download, Try, Give Feedback, and Enhance

**Opening:**
> Welcome to Percona's Community Page for Kubernetes Operators! The Percona Operators automate
> the creation, alteration, or deletion of members in your Percona Distribution for MySQL,
> MongoDB, or PostgreSQL environments. We would love your help in contributing to those projects
> and driving them forward. How can you do that? It could be very simple.

**What makes this work:**
- Immediately explains what the page is for.
- "It could be very simple" lowers the barrier without being patronizing.
- Direct question mid-paragraph keeps the reader engaged.

## Example 3: Technical opinion article

**Title:** Update your databases now to avoid data debt

**Opening:**
> 2026 should be a year of database updates, upgrades, and migrations. Across all of the most
> commonly used open source databases, end-of-life dates are forcing teams to take stock and
> move their workloads. The alternative is to stick with what is in place. While staying put
> might work in the short term, it will lead to more problems and higher costs over time.

**What makes this work:**
- Opens with a year — immediately anchors in something timely and operational.
- Acknowledges both sides before prescribing anything.
- Empathizes with the reader's operational reality before offering a path forward.

## Example 4: Community management opinion piece

**Title:** The tragedy of the divided inbox: Why "organised" communities are dying

**Opening:**
> I recently shared my views about my local tennis club's membership changes in our main
> WhatsApp group. It was relevant, timely, and affected everyone in that room. The first
> response I received? "Don't do that. Don't post it here. It's inappropriate."

**What makes this work:**
- Opens with a specific moment. Immediately human and concrete.
- Uses a non-technical example to make a point that applies across all communities.
- The quoted response creates mild friction that keeps the reader reading.

## Example 5: Rewritten community web page (MySQL)

**Opening:**
> MySQL runs at the core of a lot of production infrastructure. If you're working with it
> day-to-day, you already know where the rough edges are. This page exists so you can do
> something about them.
>
> Contributing code is one option. So is filing a well-documented bug report, answering a
> question on the forum that's been sitting unanswered, or leaving a detailed review that
> helps another DBA evaluate whether a tool fits their stack. All of it moves things forward.

**CTA example:**
> If you're new to contributing, a bug report with clear reproduction steps is as useful as a PR.
> Start there if you're not sure where to jump in.

**What makes this work:**
- "You already know where the rough edges are" — assumes operational experience without stating it.
- Specific ("clear reproduction steps"), not generic ("all contributions welcome").
- Low-barrier entry point stated plainly, without over-reassuring.

## Tone patterns to replicate

**Opening moves that work:**
- State a real operational situation the reader recognizes.
- Name a common assumption — then challenge it.
- Open with a specific moment or scenario.
- Lead with a date, a deadline, or a fact that creates urgency.

**Sentence rhythms that work:**
- Long setup. Short punch. Fragment even.
- "X is Y. Not Z." — second sentence corrects or sharpens the first.
- "If you need X, this is your path." — direct conditional, no hedging.

**Ways to address the reader:**
- "you and your team" for operational content
- "we" when sharing a collective challenge or position
- "I" in opinion pieces with a personal stance
- Never passive constructions that avoid addressing anyone

**Ways to end a section:**
- A specific next action: "Start there if you're not sure where to jump in."
- A direct recommendation: "If your availability requirements push you there, use Percona XtraDB Cluster (PXC)."
- A question that lingers: "If you have 1,000 members, why do you need 20 channels?"
